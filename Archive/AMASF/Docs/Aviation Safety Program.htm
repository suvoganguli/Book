REFUSE-TO-CRASH
NASA TACKLES LOSS OF CONTROL
Technologies under study at NASA will alert pilots of loss of control in time to take action-or take action for them.

by
John W. Croft
Aerospace America March 2003


NASA built subscale wind tunnel models to test various loss-of-control scanarios.

Technologies under study at NASA will alert pilots of loss of control in time to take action-or take action for themNASA is mobilizing its brain power to strike the last six words from an old adage in commercial aviation: An airline pilot's job involves countless hours of sheer boredom punctuated by moments of sheer terror.

It is not that NASA wants to make pilots obsolete; rather, the agency is seeking to save lives. Accident statistics show that so-called "loss-of-control" (LOC) events, infrequent situations where a pilot, for whatever reason, cannot fly straight and level, have been the primary cause of fatal U.S. commercial airline crashes for most of the last decade-and a likely source of those moments of terror.

"Refuse-to-crash" (RTC) and "self-healing" aircraft (SHA) technologies under development at NASA-Langley may be the answer. Both programs are multilayered, proactive safety nets designed to identify failed components or systems and reconfigure an aircraft to fly properly. But NASA plans to go at least one step further, giving pilots a visual display that tells them how to fly a wayward craft or even a "panic button" that would automatically untangle the vehicle from a tumble or upset.

In short, the technology could be the airline industry's solution to LOC, by far the largest lethal force in commercial aviation accidents from 1990 to 1996, accounting for more than 40% of the fatalities, according to the National Transportation Safety Board's accident database.

The NASA effort is part of the government's five-year, $500-million Aviation Safety Program, aimed at creating technology innovations that can help cut the overall commercial aviation accident rate by 80% of 1997 levels by 2007, a goal initiated by the Clinton administration and adopted by the FAA and industry as well.

NASA also has a long-term goal of reducing the accident rate by 90% by 2022. Langley is developing the RTC and SHA technologies as part of the Single Aircraft Accident Prevention (SAAP) project, headed by John J. White. In another SAAP initiative, White is working with NASA-Glenn on initiatives to make aircraft engines safer.

Although the full promise of a NASA-designed safety net that would target component failures as well as control loss is a decade or two away, interim results could soon aid airlines in meeting the 2007 safety goal. For instance, White's engineers are currently testing findings from wind tunnel work that could make flight simulators better suited to training pilots in recovery from unusual attitudes. These improvements are the result of Langley's systematic effort to define the LOC problem, model it, and provide a means to correct it.Understanding loss of control

The factors behind some types of accidents have been effectively addressed via technology. For instance, controlled flight into terrain, a major contributor to commercial aviation crashes both in the U.S. and world-wide, has been greatly reduced with the arrival of the enhanced ground proximity warning systems, now a required piece of equipment on U.S. transport aircraft. Synthetic vision system technologies-devices that enable pilots to "see" through clouds and darkness-also under development in NASA's Aviation Safety Program, will help even more. However, no "silver bullet" has yet been developed for LOC. One reason is the complex nature of the beast.

Before trying to find a solution, a research team led by Langley's Christine Belcastro, senior research engineer and technical manager of the SAAP Control Upset Prevention and Recovery (CUPR) element, now also referred to as RTC, reviewed aircraft accident data to better understand causal factors and conditions associated with recent loss-of-control accidents. They scoured other NASA technology efforts and work done for the military, held workshops with industry and other government agencies, and considered potential future causes of LOC accidents. This led to a systems-level road map of what technology areas it made sense for Belcastro and her team to pursue. Their next step was to find out what tools were needed to model and simulate an LOC event for a transport aircraft, both to study the anatomy of an upset and, later, to test preventions and countermeasures. Belcastro launched a preliminary research effort with Boeing in 1998 to survey industry upset recovery training programs, accident data, and existing transport simulation data and models.

Boeing's findings, in addition to helping shape the long-term goals, pointed out a problem area where NASA could quickly make a difference.

By looking at detailed flight-path data from a variety of commercial aviation LOC accidents, Boeing found that pilots were in many cases faced with flying an aircraft in an unusual attitude, defined by an extreme angle of attack and/or sideslip angle, where limited or no test flight or wind tunnel data exist. Since the majority of pilots find themselves in such a position only during frequent training sessions in today's highly sophisticated full-motion flight simulators, the team decided it was critical to determine if current simulators accurately represent the aircraft's handling characteristics in control-loss scenarios.

This assessment also showed that pilots had trouble understanding the fundamental causes of upsets and recovering from aerodynamic stalls (which typically coincide with an unusual attitude), especially when there were added complications such as low altitude, poor visibility, or improper control inputs arising from system faults and failures.Simulating the simulator

Normally, flight simulators are very representative of real life, with manufacturers "spending a lot of time making sure the flight simulator flies like the airplane," according to John Foster, the lead dynamics modeling expert with the RTC project. Flight-validated data are typically combined with wind tunnel data to expand a flight simulator's envelope past the normal operating regions. Outside of that, however-the region where stalls and upsets may take an aircraft-manufacturers either "hold the last value" or extrapolate to compute aircraft performance, Foster explains.

It was the "simulator envelope" and beyond that was of most concern to the research team, because if pilots were trained to counter LOC scenarios in those regions, they could potentially receive "negative training," or learn an incorrect response to an already bad situation.

To make a comparison, the team of NASA and Boeing engineers spent 1,600 "occupancy-hours" in three wind tunnels collecting aerodynamic data on two scale models of a twin-engine airliner similar to NASA's Boeing 757-200. The models were positioned for angles of attack up to 90° and sideslip angles out to 45°. In addition to static runs, where the model is rigidly mounted, the engineers ran forced oscillation and rotary balance testing with the model in motion to gather rate damping terms.

The wind tunnel results were eye-opening. In some cases, Foster found that flight simulator output was "not representative" of the aerodynamics of the airplane, meaning the validity of a flight simulator in those regions was questionable. Though significant, Foster said the findings did not reflect poorly on the airline industry: "The fidelity of airline training simulations is very good, but they were never designed to handle extreme flight conditions."

Preliminary results, verified by comparing flight data recorder information from accident aircraft, indicated that differences in handling characteristics at unusual attitudes could be quite different from what pilots have been experiencing in the flight simulator. For instance the wind-tunnel-derived coefficient of pitching moment-a measure of the aerodynamics forcing the airplane nose up and down-was drastically different from the existing simulation at an angle of attack greater than 20°. The same was true for elevator power, a measure of how much control the pilot can exert with the elevator.

Foster says the results indicate that existing flight simulators would overestimate the pitch control forces available to fly the aircraft at a tail angle of attack greater than about 24°. He expects the same results for rudder power, although the analysis is not yet finished.

The wind tunnel work had a secondary purpose as well-it would give NASA and its partners the means to assemble a realistic mathematical simulation for testing advanced detection, identification, and recovery algorithms for similar aircraft configurations.Taking a flyer

With the wind tunnel tests complete, Langley had in its possession an extensive aerodynamic data set for a large transport aircraft in virtually any orientation, and with it, the makings of an LOC model. But before improving simulators or creating LOC countermeasures, the team had to find a way to verify the wind tunnel results. The best way to do that-flying NASA's 757-200 in an out-of-control condition and comparing the results with the model-was too risky. A second alternative, using data from the flight data recorders of accident aircraft or the results of the occasional extreme in-flight test, would have been helpful, but there was only a limited amount of data available.

White and the CUPR team settled on a third, more creative option-building free-flying subscale models complete with avionics, telemetry systems, and miniature jet engines. By "flying" out-of-control scenarios with subscale transport models, the team expects to be able to validate the out-of-control aerodynamic models.

The Subscale Aircraft Testbed will include three subscale aircraft models, two as trainers and one a dynamically scaled replica of a large transport configuration, meaning the model's mass and inertia-and motion-are scaled to closely represent those of a full-scale aircraft in the flight regimes of interest. The testbed will also include a ground station where pilots can "fly" the airplane or where engineers can try out RTC algorithms in a closed-loop environment.

First flight of a trainer is expected this summer at the NASA Wallops Flight Facility in Virginia. Langley will also use the testbed to develop expanded aerodynamic envelopes for other aircraft in the next two to five years, possibly by broadening the results to a point where they apply to entire families of aircraft, for instance low-wing transport aircraft with wing-mounted engines.

The obvious question about this verification and test approach is whether the subscale model aerodynamics are representative of the real aircraft. In controlled-flight regimes-minimal angle of attack and sideslip-they are not, Foster says. But in out-of-control flight with high angle of attack and high sideslip-the area Langley wants to explore-Foster says wind tunnel results indicate that the aerodynamics will be "much closer," because the flow is separated, meaning the data are less sensitive to Reynolds number effects. [The Reynolds number is an indicator of how the airflow separates in the flow field around a model.]The end game

The larger goal for the subscale models, once the LOC models are honed, will be to prove out new algorithms to detect and correct an impending LOC situation. Current plans call for these algorithms to be packaged as an integrated failure/damage detection and accommodation system that includes either manual guidance to the pilots or a push-button automatic recovery mode.

Fault detection and identification (FDI) systems, the high-level monitors responsible for determining if an anomaly has occurred, will be based on mathematical models of the components, such as a rudder actuator, and of the aircraft's motion. For instance, if the pilot commands an airplane to fly straight and level and the craft suddenly yaws to the left, a series of component and system monitors must compare the modeled response against the aircraft's response, then apply preprogrammed rules to determine if an actuator failed, if the pilot improperly configured the aircraft, or if the aircraft is perhaps flying through an atmospheric condition such as wake turbulence.

Key to making a model-based system work effectively will be coming up with the right models and with setting the optimal detection sensitivity. If the sensitivity is too high, the failure/damage detector may assume a component has failed when in fact the aircraft is simply riding through turbulence. If there are too many false alarms, Belcastro fears that the pilots will disengage the unit as an unnecessary annoyance. The flip side is that if the sensitivity is too low, real failures will be missed.

If FDI represents the "thinking" side of the RTC hierarchy, the guidance and control sector represents the muscle power. Once the FDI detects a fault, rightly or wrongly, it will be up to the adaptive guidance and control segment to respond.

Currently, the design is based on a list of anticipated failures in such components as actuators and sensors, and will most likely start off as a limited subset of numerous possibilities. "It's impossible to have a system that's all-knowing or all-seeing," Belcastro says. Instead, the control accommodations will have to be "robust," she says, meaning the same control response would have to be generic enough to handle various failures.

That control response could be presented to the pilots in the form of a display that shows the failure and the limitations on control authority due to the failure, or it could be automated to act independently (the panic button), providing pilots with status information as the computer makes the correction.

The RTC systems will likely run in the background and provide situation awareness for the pilots, although much of the implementation scheme remains undecided, according to Belcastro. The algorithms are being developed independently by NASA-Langley, Honeywell, Lockheed Martin, Barron Associates, and other contractors and universities. By 2005, the team will begin testing preliminary FDI and guidance and control algorithms in the test facilities at Langley.Aircraft, heal thyself

Another research effort that complements and further strengthens the RTC safety net is Langley's Self-Healing Aircraft technology initiative, formally known as the Vehicle Health Management and Flight Critical Systems Design element of SAAP

Directed by Celeste Belcastro, a senior research engineer at NASA-Langley, vehicle health management is a predictive safety tool, a condition-monitoring and trend analysis system that is designed to monitor the aircraft structure and subsystems and offer a prognosis to the crew or maintenance department long before a failure occurs.

Belcastro, Christine's identical twin, is also in charge of the Flight Critical Systems Design activity, which is seeking ways to guarantee ultrahigh integrity in critical aircraft systems through advanced design and verification methods, as well as new architectures that are robust to transient electrical disturbances.

Taken together, the Belcastro projects, combined with the propulsion system safety work at NASA-Glenn, in theory should provide a near-foolproof safety net for a flight vehicle: Improve critical flight systems, use smart monitoring to predict a failure before it happens, and, failing that, isolate and accommodate the malfunction and give the pilots the means to safely deal with it.

NASA will find out what pilots think, too: White plans to bring some of them to Langley this summer to fly the simulator and evaluate upset condition handling qualities using the wind-tunnel-derived out-of-control data.

When mature, Langley's RTC and associated technologies may provide a second chance to pilots, passengers, and manufacturers for those inevitable times when boredom takes a break.
